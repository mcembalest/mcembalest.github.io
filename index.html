About Me

I am an educator currently studying machine learning

My academic interests are in interpretable and transparent algorithm design.

I like to speculate on the implications of modern technology in education and society - I think that rising levels of state debt to public pensions, combined with advances in computation, automated decision making, and surveillance, is going to be a major incentive for government investment in so-called "AI for Education" in order to reduce the number of people the government has to pay in the education sector. I am against such an initiative; children are better raised by human beings than by bureaucratic procedures, and with increased integration of automated decision making, deviations and exceptions and messiness all come with the expense of some appeal to an automated authority. With humans in the loop, we can understand particularities; but at the same time, with humans in the loop we can in other ways introduce biases that an automated process would not display. I believe in having challenging, nuanced conversations about keeping tech at bay in schools, while at the same time looking for ways that tech can increase efficiency in simple, teacher-and-student-focused manner.

The scale and opacity of modern computation should be reduced in order to give consumers easier access to information and recourse over their interactions with technology.
